Policy & Risk▼

California AB 489 (effective Jan 1, 2026) prohibits AI systems from using language or design that implies a healthcare license or that AI is a human clinician. Applies to developers and deployers. If any BVL deliverable includes AI-generated content that could be read as coming from a licensed professional, this is a compliance flag.

State legislatures are moving faster than federal. California, Colorado, and others are rolling out laws targeting AI that impersonates doctors, "algorithmic discrimination," and clinical oversight requirements. No federal comprehensive AI law yet — patchwork is the operative word for 2026.

Key risk for BVL: Clients will increasingly ask "is this AI-generated?" Transparency requirements are hardening. BVL's AI use policy and disclosure posture should be client-ready now, not reactive.

Mental health AI and minor-facing AI are drawing the most state legislative attention. If any BVL client is in behavioral health or pediatrics, prioritize reviewing their AI-adjacent content strategy.

RED-C Phase 3 Failure — Major Pipeline Blow

Competitive Intelligence▼

HE: New 2026 ACG Guidelines Reinforce Rifaximin's Role

The American College of Gastroenterology published updated HE guidelines in March 2026 (24 recommendations). Key finding: rifaximin is recommended as add-on to lactulose for patients with recurrent overt HE (OHE) episodes. This reinforces Xifaxan's clinical position in HE and provides a guideline-based sales narrative.

Sources: Guideline Central – Key Changes ↗ | Guideline Central – Spotlight ↗

HE Pipeline: MASTERPIECE Microbiome Trial

An NIH-supported trial (MASTERPIECE) is investigating oral microbiome transplant capsules for HE prevention in cirrhosis patients. Early-stage; no efficacy data yet. To watch as a longer-term alternative to rifaximin in HE prophylaxis.

Source: Guideline Central – Trial Preview ↗

IBS-D: Microbiome Therapy Shows Promise

EnteroBiotix announced positive Phase II results for EBX-102-02, an oral full-spectrum microbiome therapeutic for IBS (both IBS-C and IBS-D). Plans to publish full dataset and initiate Phase IIb for IBS-C in 2026. Still years from market, but a meaningful longer-term threat to rifaximin's microbiome-adjacent positioning.

Source: Drug Discovery World ↗

IBS-D: Rifaximin vs. Low FODMAP Diet

A study published in Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology found low FODMAP diet and rifaximin provided similar symptom relief in IBS-D, with gut microbiome characteristics predicting which patients respond to each. Strategic Signals▼

Franchise at an Inflection Point

The RED-C failure removes Bausch's most anticipated Xifaxan lifecycle extension. With the patent cliff locked in around 2028–2029 and no successor formulation now in pivotal trials, the Xifaxan franchise is entering a 3-year revenue defense window. Expect Bausch to prioritize:

  • Aggressive litigation to push generics past 2028
  • Pricing and access optimization to maximize near-term revenue
  • Potential new investigator-sponsored or label expansion efforts

ACG Guidelines = Near-Term Commercial Tailwind

The March 2026 ACG guideline update explicitly recommending rifaximin add-on to lactulose in recurrent OHE gives Salix a clear promotional hook in the HE segment. Expect this to be activated in sales and medical education efforts.

IBS-D Microbiome Science Shifting

The convergence of microbiome-based data (EnteroBiotix, FODMAP/rifaximin comparison study) signals that IBS-D treatment narratives are shifting toward personalized, microbiome-informed approaches. Xifaxan's mechanism fits this story, but Salix will need to proactively engage on this science to maintain positioning.

No Immediate Threat, But Watch Amneal

The most likely near-term disruption is Amneal resolving or settling litigation and launching an authorized generic earlier than 2028. A settlement in the next 12–18 months is possible given the Teva/Sun/Sandoz precedents.
