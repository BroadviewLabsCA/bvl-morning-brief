Competitive Intelligence▼

Hepatic Encephalopathy

2026 ACG guideline reinforces rifaximin's clinical position. Published March 2026, the American College of Gastroenterology's updated HE guideline explicitly recommends adding rifaximin to lactulose for patients with recurrent overt HE on maintenance therapy. This is a direct positive for Xifaxan's near-term positioning, as it validates the existing indication and may support pull-through.

  • Source: ACG Clinical Guideline: Hepatic Encephalopathy — March 2026 ↗
  • Source: Hepatic Encephalopathy Guidelines 2026 Update — Guideline Central ↗

HE pipeline — microbiome and albumin approaches advancing. Four Phase 2 trials completing 2026–2030:

  • MASTERPIECE (VA-sponsored): Microbiota transplant capsules to prevent recurrent HE. Starting Oct 2026, completion Oct 2030. 162 patients.
  • HEAL-LAST: IV albumin for cognitive improvement in cirrhosis patients with prior HE. Completing Oct 2026.
  • FMT trial (Mass General): Fecal microbiota transplant oral capsules vs. placebo. Completing Dec 2026.

None of these are near-term commercial threats but the microbiome space is directionally competitive with rifaximin's mechanism.

  • Source: HE Trials Preview — Guideline Central (May 5, 2026) ↗

IBS-D

Viberzi (eluxadoline) remains constrained by safety restrictions (sphincter of Oddi spasm risk), limiting uptake among prescribers. Not a growing threat to Xifaxan share.

Linzess (linaclotide) targets IBS-C, not IBS-D — not a direct competitor. US net sales ~$864.5M in 2025.

Ibsrela (tenapanor) also targets IBS-C. $274.2M in 2025, up 73% YoY. Growing fast but in a different indication lane.

Patent & Legal Watch▼

Generic window: ~2029. Xifaxan patent exclusivity extends through 2028–2029. Alvogen's challenge was blocked in 2024, resetting the competitive clock.

Amneal: FDA tentative approval granted for rifaximin 550mg (IBS-D indication). Amneal filed an ANDA; Bausch Health / Salix responded with a Hatch-Waxman lawsuit, triggering a 30-month stay on FDA final approval. FDA has since granted tentative approval — meaning Amneal cannot market until the patent dispute is resolved or the stay expires. This is the most active generic front.

Additional ANDA filers (including Norwich Pharmaceuticals) face similar litigation. Bausch Health is defending across multiple fronts.

Strategic Signals▼

Bausch's pipeline is thinner than expected. SSD rifaximin failure removes the primary post-Xifaxan growth narrative heading into the 2029 patent cliff. Remaining pipeline: larsucosterol (alcohol-associated hepatitis), amiselimod (ulcerative colitis, under internal review), Thermage FLX (dermatology). None replicate Xifaxan's scale.

Near-term revenue looks healthy; mid-term is exposed. Q1 2026 Xifaxan growth of 21% is strong and suggests commercial execution is intact. But with SSD rifaximin gone and generics on a defined timeline, the company faces a revenue gap risk around 2029–2030 without a new anchor product.

ACG guideline is a near-term tailwind. The March 2026 guideline's explicit recommendation to add rifaximin to lactulose strengthens the clinical case for prescribing Xifaxan in HE — potentially expanding pull-through from hepatologists following updated protocols.

Generic Amneal pressure is building but legally contained. Tentative FDA approval for Amneal's ANDA is a signal that regulatory science is in order — the only barrier is Bausch's Hatch-Waxman litigation. If courts find against Bausch on any key patent, the timeline could accelerate.

Microbiome approaches are the long-range watch. FMT and microbiota transplant capsules are in early Phase 2 for HE. If Phase 2 data is positive, this could be a meaningful mechanism-level challenge to rifaximin's gut-targeted antibacterial approach by the early 2030s.
